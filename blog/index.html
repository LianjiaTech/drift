DRIFT is an open-source online self-evolution policy optimization framework designed to empower large language models with continuous reasoning improvements without relying on external expert supervision.

Traditional self-distillation and RL alignment paradigms often treat model generation trajectories statically and fail to dynamically model the learning progress. This frequently results in redundant computation on simple questions, lack of direction on complex prompts, and sparse exploration at logical boundaries.

By introducing dynamic Difficulty Routing, a robust Success Buffer, and token-level Rhythm Gating, DRIFT builds an adaptive, structured exploration loop that yields stable and scalable policy improvement across diverse disciplines.

Overview

Enabling large language models to stably self-evolve without external expert supervision remains a central challenge in complex reasoning and scientific problem solving. Existing self-distillation and reinforcement learning methods often treat model-generated trajectories as static supervision, without explicitly modeling learning progress or dynamically adjusting training strategies. This can lead to over-optimization on easy problems, insufficient guidance on hard problems, and inadequate exploration on boundary cases. To address these issues, we propose DRIFT, an online self-evolutionary policy optimization framework for large language models.

Our contributions are as follows:

  1. Rhythm-gated structured exploration. We design a structure-preserving exploration mechanism that encourages policy variation near the success boundary, while preserving useful reasoning patterns through token-level Rhythm gating.
  2. Difficulty routing Optimizer. We introduce a difficulty routing mechanism that uses historical pass rates as a stable estimate of the model's mastery level. This mechanism dynamically determines when to distill from past successful trajectories, when to explore new strategies, and when to reduce redundant training.
  3. Two-stage curriculum learning. We propose a two-stage curriculum learning strategy. In the first stage, an efficient SDPO warm-up rapidly improves model pass@k while accumulating a success buffer. In the second stage, SDPO is combined with GRPO to balance exploration, correction, and stable convergence, enabling continuous and stable performance improvement.
  4. Stable self-evolution with success buffer. DRIFT incorporates a historical success memory, namely the success buffer, to reuse high-quality trajectories. It further adopts a curriculum learning strategy to support smooth model evolution and uses Rhythm gating to provide structured exploration rewards on boundary problems.

We evaluate DRIFT on diverse task categories, including biology, chemistry, materials science, physics, and tool use. Experimental results show that DRIFT significantly outperforms reproduced SDPO and GRPO baselines, and further improves upon recent methods such as SRPO, demonstrating stronger training stability and better cross-domain generalization.

Methodology

  • Rhythm-Gated Structured Exploration. Standard GRPO applies the same trajectory-level advantage to all tokens, regardless of their importance within the reasoning process. As a result, exploration signals may be diluted by semantically uninformative tokens, leading to noisy credit assignment. To improve exploration efficiency, we introduce Rhythm-Gated Structured Exploration, a token-level weighting mechanism built on top of the GRPO branch. The key idea is to amplify policy updates only when a token simultaneously exhibits strong structural significance and demonstrates local improvement relative to the teacher. The resulting rhythm gate $M^{\mathrm{rhy}}_{i,t}$ selectively enhances exploration at critical reasoning positions while suppressing unnecessary perturbations elsewhere. This leads to more targeted exploration and more efficient policy refinement near the success boundary.
  • Difficulty Routing System. We introduce a difficulty routing system that dynamically adjusts the reinforcement learning weight according to problem mastery. Specifically, we maintain a smoothed pass-rate estimate for each problem using both historical and current training statistics. Based on the estimated pass rate, problems are categorized into easy, medium, and hard regions, and different values of $\gamma_i$ are assigned accordingly. This routing strategy concentrates reinforcement learning updates on medium-difficulty problems, where policy improvement is typically the most effective.
  • Two-stage curriculum learning. DRIFT adopts a two-stage curriculum learning strategy. In the warm-up stage, we primarily employ SDPO, which is significantly more sample-efficient than GRPO and can rapidly improve the model's performance. After the model reaches a reasonable performance level, we switch to a hybrid training stage that combines self-distillation and GRPO. Self-distillation provides corrective supervision for failed trajectories, whereas GRPO further improves successful trajectories through reward-driven optimization.
  • Success Buffer. To improve the utilization of successful trajectories, we introduce a Success Buffer that stores high-quality historical rollouts throughout training. Unlike standard on-policy optimization, where successful trajectories are discarded after a single update, our framework preserves them as reusable teacher signals for future self-distillation. During training, if no successful sibling trajectory is available within the current rollout group, a teacher trajectory is retrieved from the corresponding success buffer and used as the privileged feedback $f_i$. To ensure that the buffer evolves together with the policy, stored trajectories are continuously updated using a reward-based replacement strategy. This mechanism alleviates success-sample sparsity and provides more stable supervision across training iterations with negligible memory overhead.

Mathematical Formulations

The overall hybrid training objective minimizes a difficulty-routed combination of self-distillation on incorrect trajectories and rhythm-gated GRPO on correct trajectories:

$$ \min\quad \mathcal{L}(\theta):= \mathbb{E}_{y_i\sim\pi_{\theta}(\cdot\mid x)} \left[ \underbrace{ \mathbf{1}_{y_i\in\mathrm{incorrect}} \,\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{SDPO}}(y_i,f_i) }_{\text{Self-Distillation}}- \underbrace{ \gamma_i\, \mathbf{1}_{y_i\in\mathrm{correct}} \,\mathcal{J}_{\mathrm{GRPO}}^{\mathrm{rhythm}}(y_i) }_{\text{Difficulty-Routed RL}} \right] $$

For more detailed formulations, please refer to our upcoming paper.
